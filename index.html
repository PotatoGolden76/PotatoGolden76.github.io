The life of Alan Turing

  Widely considered to be the father of theoretical computer science and artificial intelligence, Alan Mathison Turing was a British mathematician, cryptanalyst, computer scientist and philosopher that led the Hut 8 section of the Government Code and Cypher School at Bletchley Park during World War II.
  The Hut 8 branch of the GC&CS was the one tasked with breaking the German naval Enigma messages. While a part of it, Turing developed a number of methods for speeding up the breaking of German ciphers, the most notable of which being the improvements made to the pre-war Polish bomba kryptologiczna machine.
  It is estimated that Alan Turing’s work shortened the war by at least 2 years and saved the lives of more than 15 million people. In spite of this, he was not fully recognised in the United Kingdom due to his homosexuality and because most of his work was covered by the "Official Secrets Act", a term used for legislation that is meant to protect state secrets and official information.
  Following the war, Alan Turing worked at the National Physical Laboratory, where he designed the Automatic Computing Engine, one of the first stored-program computers. In 1948 he joined Max Newman's Computing Machine Laboratory, at the Victoria University of Manchester, where he helped develop the Manchester computers.
